What They Teach You

Philip Delves Broughton · Harvard Business School · Class of 2006

Broughton was a correspondent for the Daily Telegraph when he decided to apply to HBS almost on a whim. He got in. What he found — and what he wrote about — is the most honest account of what a top-tier MBA actually does to a person, and what it genuinely delivers.

Book I · Chapter 1
01

The Machine You're Buying Into

What HBS actually is, how it works, and what you're really paying $200,000 for
The Foundation

Harvard Business School was founded in 1908 with a radical idea: that management could be taught as a profession, the way law or medicine was taught. It was a controversial bet. Most people believed business competence was innate — you either had the instincts or you didn't. HBS said: we can build it.

Today, it is the world's most mythologized institution of business learning. And here is the critical distinction Broughton makes immediately: HBS is a credentialing machine as much as it is a knowledge machine. Getting in already signals that you are smart, driven, and connected enough to clear an extraordinarily competitive filter. What happens after you're in is partly education — and partly confirmation bias working relentlessly in your favor.

The HBS brand does not just open doors. It makes people assume you deserve to walk through them — before you've said a word.
— Broughton's core observation
01
The Case Method — HBS's Core Technology
Every class is a real business situation. You form a judgment before you enter the room. Then you defend it in front of 90 people.
▾ expand
How it works: Each class is built around a real business situation — 15 to 25 pages of dense, carefully researched narrative about an actual company facing an actual decision. You read it the night before. You form a position. The next morning, a professor cold-calls you in front of 90 peers, and you defend or collapse.

The genius: You learn by deciding, not by being told. The professor almost never gives you the answer. The answer — if it exists — emerges through argument, contradiction, and collective stress-testing. This trains a very specific cognitive muscle: forming confident judgments under ambiguity, with incomplete information, under social pressure. That is exactly what senior executives do every day.

The danger: The case method rewards articulation over accuracy. It rewards sounding decisive over being right. Some of the most brilliant case performers at HBS went on to spectacular failures. Some of the quiet ones became the most consequential leaders of their generation. The school knows this and acknowledges it imperfectly.
02
The Shadow Curriculum Nobody Puts in the Brochure
Beyond Finance, Strategy, and Marketing — there's a second curriculum that runs invisibly beneath the official one.
▾ expand
Network as Infrastructure: Your 900 classmates will scatter to every corner of the institutional economy. Ten years later, your cold call to a Goldman partner is a warm call because you sat three rows behind her in Finance. The relationship network accumulated over two years is the single most durable and valuable thing the school produces.

Manufactured Confidence: HBS produces certainty. You graduate believing you can lead anything — a startup, a hospital, a country. This is partly the school's greatest gift and partly its most dangerous product. The confidence is real. The calibration of that confidence to actual competence is inconsistent.

Pattern Recognition at Scale: 500 cases means your brain has matched patterns across 500 different industries, crises, and decisions. A supply chain crisis in Indonesian textiles rhymes with one in pharmaceutical distribution. A new situation snaps into a recognizable category. You become fast to competence in unfamiliar domains. This is genuinely valuable and genuinely hard to get any other way.

The Brand as Perpetual Signal: Long after graduation, the two letters on your résumé continue doing work. Board committees, search firms, venture funds — they all run on cognitive shortcuts. "Harvard MBA" is a shortcut that opens rooms, activates trust, and greases introductions in ways that pure competence, without the signal, would not.

Finance & the Numbers That Don't Lie

How HBS teaches you to read money — and why most educated people still can't
Core Curriculum

The first thing HBS does — week one, before you have settled into your section, before you have learned your professors' names — is make sure every student can read a financial statement. Poet, engineer, army general: it does not matter. If you cannot read the numbers, you cannot participate.

And not just read them — interrogate them. Numbers do not lie, but they omit. The skill is not knowing what the numbers say. The skill is knowing what they're hiding, what they're assuming, and what question you need to ask to expose the gap between accounting reality and business reality.

◆ The Three Financial Documents Every Decision-Maker Must Master
Income Statement
What happened over a period. Revenue flows in, costs flow out, profit (or loss) remains. The top line is revenue. The bottom line is net income. Neither tells you the full story alone. A company can have record revenue and be losing money. A company can have shrinking revenue and be extraordinarily profitable. Read income statements in context, not in isolation.
Balance Sheet
What exists at a point in time. Assets (what you own) on one side; liabilities (what you owe) and equity (what's left for shareholders) on the other. The balance sheet is a snapshot. It tells you whether the company is solvent, how leveraged it is, and how efficiently it's deploying capital. A healthy income statement on an unhealthy balance sheet is a ticking clock.
Cash Flow Statement
The only number that doesn't lie. Cash in minus cash out. A company can be "profitable" by accounting definitions while simultaneously running out of cash — because profit includes non-cash items like depreciation and deferred revenue. Amazon ran cash-negative for years while reporting operating profits. Companies don't go bankrupt because they're unprofitable. They go bankrupt because they run out of cash.
Financial Metrics Decoded
Gross MarginRevenue minus direct cost of goods. Reveals pricing power and production efficiency. High gross margin = room to absorb overhead and still profit.
EBITDAEarnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization. Strips out financing and accounting decisions to show raw operational performance. Used universally in valuation.
Free Cash FlowOperating cash flow minus capital expenditures. The purest measure of value creation. The number Warren Buffett obsesses over.
ROICReturn on invested capital. Is this business generating returns above its cost of capital? If yes, it's creating value. If no, it's destroying it — regardless of what the P&L says.
Working CapitalCurrent assets minus current liabilities. Measures short-term financial health. A company with shrinking working capital is approaching a liquidity squeeze.
03
The Three Lenses of Valuation
What is a business worth? Three methodologies, each with a different philosophical premise. Professionals use all three to triangulate.
▾ expand
DCF — Discounted Cash Flow (Intrinsic Value): Project every dollar of free cash flow the business will generate in the future. Discount those future dollars back to today's value using a rate that reflects the riskiness of the business. Sum them up. That's what the business is worth today if your projections are correct. The problem: tiny changes in your growth rate assumption or discount rate produce wildly different valuations. DCF is philosophically rigorous and practically unreliable. Use it as an anchor, not an oracle.

Comparable Company Analysis (Market Value): What are similar businesses trading at in the public market? If competitors trade at 12× EBITDA and your company generates $50M EBITDA, the market would value you around $600M. This is market-derived and practical — but circular. It assumes the market is correctly pricing those comparable companies, which may or may not be true.

Precedent Transaction Analysis (Acquisition Value): What have acquirers actually paid for similar businesses in recent M&A deals? Buyers pay a control premium — typically 20–40% above market price — to gain operational control. This method gives you the most realistic picture of what someone would actually write you a check for.

The professional approach: Run all three. Where they converge, you have confidence. Where they diverge, you investigate why — the divergence itself is intelligence about the business.
Accounting profit is an opinion. Cash flow is a fact. Build your analysis around the fact, and use the opinion to understand why the fact looks the way it does.
— Core HBS Finance Principle
04
Capital Structure — How You Finance Changes What You Risk
Debt is cheaper than equity. It's also a fixed obligation. The optimal capital structure maximizes return without making the company fragile.
▾ expand
Why debt is "cheaper" than equity: Debt interest is tax-deductible. Equity dividends are not. If your cost of debt is 6% and your tax rate is 30%, the effective cost is 4.2%. Meanwhile, equity investors expect returns of 10–15% minimum. So debt is mathematically cheaper — but only as long as you can service it.

The risk tradeoff: Debt is a fixed obligation. Miss a payment and you're in technical default. Equity is forgiving — you can skip dividends indefinitely without legal consequence. The more debt you carry, the higher your potential equity return (leverage amplifies gains) — and the higher your risk of catastrophic failure (leverage amplifies losses identically).

Private Equity as applied science: PE firms use this asymmetry deliberately. They acquire a business using mostly borrowed money (leveraged buyouts), meaning a small equity slice controls a large asset. If the business generates sufficient cash to service the debt and grows, the equity return is extraordinary. This is not magic — it's financial engineering applied to businesses with predictable, stable cash flows. PE firms love boring, unglamorous businesses for exactly this reason. Exciting, unpredictable businesses cannot reliably service debt. Boring ones can.

Strategy — The War for Position

Porter, competitive advantage, disruption theory, and why most "strategic plans" are just budgets with ambition attached
Core Curriculum

Michael Porter — an HBS professor — invented the modern language of competitive strategy. Before Porter, strategy was largely intuitive: you competed by being better. Porter asked a different question: Why are some industries structurally more profitable than others, regardless of how well individual companies are managed?

That question changed everything. The answer is that industry structure determines the average profitability ceiling. Being excellent in a structurally awful industry still leaves you fighting for thin margins. Being mediocre in a structurally excellent industry still produces good profits. Choose your battlefield as carefully as you execute within it.

◆ Porter's Five Forces — The Industry Profitability Map
Threat of New Entrants
If anyone can enter your market cheaply and easily, competition will erode your margins until they approach zero. High barriers — capital intensity, regulatory licensing, brand loyalty, network effects, patents — protect incumbent profits indefinitely. The best businesses are the ones newcomers can't copy, even when they can see exactly how profitable it is.
Supplier Power
If your suppliers have pricing power over you — because you depend on them more than they depend on you — they extract your margins systematically. Single-source dependency is a structural vulnerability. Diversify supply or vertically integrate when supplier concentration becomes a strategic risk.
Buyer Power
If your customers can walk and your product isn't differentiated enough to retain them, they extract margin through negotiation. Buyer concentration (a few large customers representing most of your revenue) is particularly lethal. One customer representing 40% of your revenue is not a relationship. It's a hostage situation.
Threat of Substitutes
Even if no direct competitor exists, something else might solve the customer's underlying problem differently. Taxis didn't compete with other taxis — Uber didn't build a better taxi. It made the concept of hailing a taxi obsolete. Always define your competitive threat by the customer's job-to-be-done, not by your product category.
Competitive Rivalry
Intense price competition among existing players destroys industry margins for everyone. Stable oligopolies — like commercial aircraft manufacturing (effectively a Boeing-Airbus duopoly) — generate extraordinary profits for decades because both players are rational enough to avoid price wars. The best competitive environments are those where rivals compete on quality and service, not price.
Generic Strategy 01
Cost Leadership
Be the lowest-cost producer in your industry. Walmart. Ryanair. IKEA. You don't need to be the best — you need to be the cheapest while remaining profitable. Requires relentless operational discipline, economies of scale, and often supply chain ownership. The trap: racing to the bottom when competitors match your cost cuts.
Generic Strategy 02
Differentiation
Be so distinct that customers pay a premium rather than accept a substitute. Apple. Ferrari. LVMH. The premium must exceed the cost of being different. Differentiation lives in product, brand, experience — but must be perceived as valuable by customers, not just declared valuable by you.
Generic Strategy 03
Focus / Niche
Serve a narrow segment so completely that you become the obvious, irreplaceable choice within that niche — even if you can't compete across the whole market. Niche firms often generate higher margins than broad players because they face less competition in their specific sandbox.
The Trap
Stuck in the Middle
Companies that attempt cost leadership and differentiation simultaneously — without committing fully to either — end up in no-man's-land. Not cheap enough for price-sensitive buyers. Not distinctive enough for premium buyers. This is the graveyard of business school strategic plans that sounded good in the classroom.
05
Disruption Theory — Christensen's Uncomfortable Truth
Incumbents rarely get killed by better competitors. They get killed by worse ones who start at the bottom and climb.
▾ expand
Clayton Christensen — another HBS legend — introduced Disruptive Innovation theory, and it remains the most useful framework for understanding why dominant companies get destroyed by seemingly inferior entrants.

The mechanism: A new entrant enters the market at the low end — serving customers the incumbent has abandoned as unprofitable, with a product that's worse by all existing metrics of quality. The incumbent is rational to ignore this: defending their premium customers is far more profitable in the short term. But the disruptor's cost structure, built for the low end, gradually improves. The technology matures. The product moves upmarket. By the time the incumbent notices the threat, the disruptor has the cost advantages, the customer base, and the technology platform to compete everywhere.

Classic patterns: Digital cameras entered below film quality — then destroyed film. Netflix started with DVD-by-mail (worse than Blockbuster for impulse renters) — then destroyed video rental. Android phones were worse than Nokia's — then made Nokia irrelevant. In every case, the disruptor was initially worse on the incumbent's quality metrics. In every case, the incumbent rationally ignored them until it was too late.

The strategic implication: If you're an incumbent, the threat is not from the competitor who's directly challenging you with a better version of your product. It's from the one building an inferior product for customers you don't care about. If you're a new entrant, the way in is not to build a premium product that competes head-on — it's to serve the market the incumbent has abandoned, then climb.
Strategy is not a list of initiatives. It's a coherent set of choices about what you will NOT do — and those choices must reinforce each other to create a position competitors cannot easily copy.
— Porter's definition, distilled

Leadership — The Module HBS Can't Fully Teach

What the school attempts, what it actually delivers, and the dangerous overconfidence it manufactures
Hardest Module

Leadership is the module HBS is least equipped to teach — and it knows this. You can teach finance through numbers. You can teach strategy through frameworks. But leadership is fundamentally about reading and moving other human beings — and you don't acquire that skill by analyzing cases about Jack Welch or studying Bill George's authentic leadership framework, however good those frameworks are.

HBS teaches leadership by dissecting what great leaders did, reverse-engineering their decisions, and identifying transferable patterns. The implicit assumption: if you understand the pattern, you can replicate the outcome. The problem: context, personality, timing, and chemistry between specific humans are not transferable the way a financial model is.

Framework
Authentic Leadership
Bill George's framework — and arguably HBS's most influential leadership export. Know who you are. Operate from clear values. Build trust through consistency. Authenticity is not being nice or transparent — it is being predictably, reliably yourself so that people can model you, trust you, and follow you without second-guessing your motives.
Framework
Situational Leadership
Different situations demand different styles. A startup in crisis needs directive, decisive, fast leadership. A mature organization in stable conditions needs collaborative, consensus-building, culture-sustaining leadership. The failure mode — applying the same style regardless of context — is one of the most common ways talented leaders destroy organizations.
Underrated Insight
Managing Upward & Sideways
Most people believe leadership is about managing downward — directing your team. The reality: real power in organizations moves laterally and upward. Your relationship with your boss, your peers, your board — these are strategic assets. The executive who is brilliant with their team but invisible to their organization's power structure is perpetually under-resourced.
Broughton's Warning
The Overconfidence Trap
HBS's most dangerous product: graduates who are supremely confident in their own frameworks. The 2008 financial crisis was designed and executed by people with elite MBAs who were certain their models were right. Epistemic humility — treating your model as a map, not the territory — is the antidote, and HBS teaches it inconsistently.
The HBS Leadership Operating System
Self-AwarenessKnow your values, your triggers, your blind spots. Leaders who don't know themselves cannot be known by others — and cannot be trusted.
VisionArticulate a future state that is vivid enough to be motivating and specific enough to be actionable. Vague vision is just management aesthetics.
CommunicationMake complex things simple. Make abstract things concrete. Make distant things personal. Three different skills that most leaders only partially develop.
Decision QualityJudge yourself on process, not outcome. Good decisions can produce bad outcomes (bad luck). Bad decisions can produce good outcomes (dumb luck). A good process is the only thing you control.
AccountabilityOwn results publicly. Distribute credit generously. Absorb blame that falls within your sphere. This asymmetry builds trust faster than almost any other leadership behavior.
ResilienceFailures are information. Process them, extract the lesson, and move. Leaders who treat failure as identity instead of information become paralyzed or defensive at exactly the wrong moments.

Marketing — The Science of Desire

Customers don't buy what you sell. They buy what it does for them. Understanding the gap is the entire game.
Core Curriculum

HBS marketing starts with a question that sounds obvious but isn't: What is your customer actually buying? Not what you're selling. What they're buying.

A drill bit manufacturer sells drill bits. Customers don't want drill bits. They want holes in walls. But even deeper: they want hung shelves, which means organized homes, which means control over their space, which means a certain feeling about their life. The job-to-be-done is the actual unit of competitive analysis — not the product category. Companies that compete on product features are fighting in a narrow space. Companies that compete on the underlying job can innovate across entirely different delivery mechanisms.

◆ STP — The Marketing Strategy Framework
Segmentation
Divide the heterogeneous market into homogeneous groups — people who share characteristics and behaviors. Geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioral — the more precisely you segment, the more precisely you can serve. Mass marketing is only viable for commodity products where differentiation is structurally impossible.
Targeting
Choose which segments to serve — and explicitly which ones not to serve. The discipline of choosing who you are NOT serving is where most companies fail. Trying to be relevant to everyone makes you unmemorable to anyone. The segments you decline are as strategically significant as the segments you pursue.
Positioning
The mental slot you occupy in your customer's mind. Positioning is not what you do to a product — it's what you do to the prospect's perception. Volvo owns safety. BMW owns driving. Volvo does not need to have the safest car on the market — it needs customers to believe it does. These positions took decades and billions to build, and would take catastrophic mismanagement to erase.
You do not create value in a factory and deliver it to customers. You create the conditions for customers to create value for themselves. Marketing is the architecture of those conditions.
— The HBS Marketing Principle
06
Brand as Financial Asset — Not Aesthetic Exercise
HBS forces students to think about brand in financial terms. Brand value is the measurable premium customers pay over an unbranded equivalent.
▾ expand
Coca-Cola is water, sugar, caramel coloring, and flavoring. It costs less than $0.05 to produce a can's worth of ingredients. The brand turns that into a $2–5 product, globally, across 200 countries, for over 130 years. The brand premium — the gap between product cost and selling price beyond what competition would allow — is pure accumulated brand value.

Brand is built by: repeated positive experiences, consistent tone and visual identity, clear positioning that doesn't drift, and the ability to stand for something specific without flinching when challenged. Every deviation from the promise chips away at the brand. Every delivery on the promise reinforces it.

Brand is destroyed by: inconsistency (saying one thing, doing another), over-extension (putting the brand name on products that dilute its meaning), credibility gaps (promising luxury and delivering mediocrity), and discounting (training customers to wait for sales, which says the real price is the sale price).

The financial implication: Investing in brand is investing in a long-duration asset that doesn't appear on the balance sheet but generates measurable revenue premium indefinitely. Cutting brand investment to boost short-term earnings is the equivalent of selling off equipment — you're converting a productive asset into current revenue at the cost of future capacity.

Ethics, Failure & Broughton's Verdict

What HBS does when it confronts its own limitations — and whether the price is worth it

HBS has a mandatory ethics curriculum — a module called LEAD (Leadership and Corporate Accountability). Broughton's account of it is damning in the most honest way. The cases are well-constructed. The frameworks are useful. The professors are serious. But the environment the ethics course exists within systematically undermines it.

The highest-paying job offers go to investment banking and private equity. The most celebrated alumni work in finance. The social prestige flows toward those who maximize financial returns. Ethics, taught in the abstract, is elbowed out by the very culture it tries to counteract. This is not hypocrisy — it's an honest reflection of the market HBS serves.

The 2008 financial crisis was the case study HBS never wrote. Designed by MBAs. Executed by MBAs. Defended by MBAs. The architects were supremely confident in their models and spectacularly, catastrophically wrong.
— Broughton's sharpest indictment
Verdict
Is a Harvard MBA worth $200,000?
For certain paths — unambiguously yes. Private equity, investment banking, top management consulting, the C-suite of major corporations: the HBS brand is nearly a prerequisite and the network is genuinely irreplaceable. The ROI, calculated over a 20-year career, is real and documented.

For startups — deeply questionable. The opportunity cost is two years and $200,000. Most successful founders who attended HBS report the most valuable element was the network, not the curriculum. You can learn the curriculum from books. You cannot replicate the network from books. If you're going to build a company, the question is whether you need the network badly enough to pay the price.

For wisdom — wrong institution. HBS produces sharpened operators, not philosophers. If you want intellectual transformation, moral depth, or genuine human development — there are better and far cheaper ways to get there. HBS optimizes for one specific objective: career trajectory in the institutional economy. It does this with extraordinary precision. Know what you're buying.

What They Don't Teach You

Mark H. McCormack · Founded IMG · Built an empire on a handshake

McCormack never went to business school. He went to William & Mary and Yale Law. Then he built IMG — representing Arnold Palmer, Tiger Woods, managing Wimbledon — not through frameworks but through four decades of reading people and situations with frightening accuracy. This book is those four decades, distilled.

Book II · Chapter 1
07

Reading People — The Foundational Edge

McCormack's most important skill — and the one no classroom has ever reliably taught
Street Intelligence

McCormack's most important skill — the one he credits for IMG's very existence — is acute, systematic observation of other human beings. Not listening to what they say. Watching what they do: how they hold themselves, what they react to, what they flinch at, what they move toward without realizing it.

He describes sitting across from potential clients and reading them like instruments. Posture when a specific topic comes up. Response time before answering. What they ask about versus what they conspicuously avoid. The moment their face changes and they don't notice it changing. This is not manipulation — it is empathy made operational. The better you understand what someone actually wants, as opposed to what they are negotiating for, the more precisely you can structure something that satisfies both parties simultaneously.

◆ McCormack's Four Human Drivers — What Actually Moves People in Business
Ego & Recognition
The single most underestimated driver in business. Most decisions — even apparently rational financial ones — are fundamentally driven by how the decision makes the decision-maker look to people they care about. The logical analysis presented in meetings is frequently post-hoc rationalization of an ego-driven choice made before the meeting started. Identify what someone's ego is invested in and you understand their behavior.
Fear of Loss
Loss aversion is more powerful than gain appetite — Kahneman proved this; McCormack knew it instinctively decades earlier. People work harder to avoid losing $1,000 than to gain $1,000. Structure your propositions around protecting what they already have — their status, their relationships, their reputation, their existing wins — and you'll close faster than any features pitch.
Belonging & Trust
People do business with people they like and trust. A slightly inferior product from a trusted partner beats a superior product from a stranger, every time, in every market. Building genuine trust — slowly, through consistency, through showing up — is a long-term competitive advantage that cannot be purchased, expedited, or faked indefinitely. McCormack built IMG on relationships that lasted decades because he treated them as the asset, not as the means to an asset.
Self-Interest
Always, always understand the other party's actual self-interest — not what they claim their interest is, but what would actually make their life better. Their career incentives. Their bonus structure. Their boss's priorities. Their personal ambitions that have nothing to do with the deal on the table. Align your proposal with their self-interest, make this alignment visible, and it almost sells itself.
McCormack's People-Reading Checklist — Run This in Every Meeting
First topic chosenWhat did they choose to talk about first? That's their priority, not small talk. File it.
Visible reactionsWhat did they physically react to? A micro-expression, a lean forward, a tone shift — these reveal true valuation before the brain can censor it.
The silencesWhat do they NOT ask about? The absence of a question is often more revealing than its presence. They avoided it for a reason.
Subordinate treatmentHow do they treat people below them in the hierarchy? This is the real character, not the performance they put on for peers and superiors.
Word/body alignmentDo their words and body language align? Incongruence — calm voice, tight posture — signals something important is unsaid.
Ego investmentWhat is their identity bound up in? Their title? Their track record? Their taste? Their reputation with a specific person? That's your key.
R1
First Impressions as Intelligence — Not Prejudice
McCormack's counterintuitive rule: form a strong first impression, then spend the next 30 minutes trying to disprove it.
▾ expand
Your gut reaction to a person in the first few seconds encodes pattern-matched intelligence your conscious mind hasn't processed yet. This is not magic — it's your brain running a fast, low-resolution pattern match against every similar person you've encountered before. It's often wrong. But it's not random noise.

McCormack's rule: take the first impression seriously as a hypothesis. Then spend the first 30 minutes of a meeting actively trying to disprove it. Ask questions that would contradict it. Watch for evidence that challenges it. If you cannot disprove it by the end of the meeting, trust it. If you can, update to the new evidence.

This is Bayesian reasoning applied to social intelligence. It avoids two failure modes: dismissing the first impression as "mere gut feeling" (losing real intelligence), and treating it as settled truth (calcifying a potentially wrong model). Most excellent operators do this instinctively. No MBA program teaches it explicitly — McCormack does.

Selling — The Skill Everyone Uses and Nobody Admits To

McCormack's complete philosophy of persuasion, from first contact to signed deal
Street Intelligence

McCormack's most radical claim, stated flatly in the book's opening chapters: selling is not a job function. It is the fundamental activity of business life. When you pitch your boss on a new strategy, you are selling. When you recruit a candidate, you are selling. When you ask for budget, you are selling. When you have been building a relationship with a client for 18 months before ever asking for business, you are selling.

The MBA who thinks selling is beneath them — a tactical activity delegated to salespeople while "real" executives think and plan — is functionally illiterate in the most important skill of organizational life. Every interaction is a sale. The only question is whether you're doing it well or poorly.

Principle 01
Sell to the Decision-Maker
Organizations decide through individuals, not committees. The committee discusses. One person decides. That person is often not the biggest title in the room. Find them. Read them. Speak to their specific concerns. Committee dynamics are noise around this core signal.
Principle 02
Believe It or Stop
McCormack refused to represent people or products he didn't genuinely believe in. His reasoning: authentic enthusiasm is unbeatable in a sales context, and faking it long-term is exhausting, eventually transparent, and ultimately corrosive to the relationship infrastructure your career runs on.
Principle 03
Silence as a Weapon
After making your pitch, stop talking. Most salespeople — nervous, eager, uncertain — fill silence with more pitching, diluting everything that came before. Silence creates pressure. It forces the other party to respond. McCormack sat in silence after proposals with patience that made counterparts visibly uncomfortable. Then they moved.
Principle 04
Real vs. Manufactured Urgency
Artificial urgency destroys trust in a single transaction. Real urgency — a genuine deadline, a competing offer, a window that closes — should be communicated clearly and factually. If there's no real urgency, paint the cost of inaction: what does the client lose by waiting six months? Make inaction feel expensive.
Find out what someone wants more than anything else. Then show them how working with you gets them there. Not what you're offering — what they want. These are often completely different conversations, and only one of them closes deals.
— McCormack's definition of great selling
R2
Medium Strategy — The Channel Determines the Message
Never negotiate a critical deal by letter. You lose everything except the words. Face-to-face is where deals actually get done.
▾ expand
McCormack was decades ahead of his time on communication strategy. His medium hierarchy, from most to least powerful for closing things that matter:

Face-to-face: The only channel for important decisions. You get everything — verbal content, tone, body language, reaction, the flinches, the openings, the moments the other party reveals what they actually want. No other channel gives you this intelligence.

Phone: Second-best. You lose body language but retain tone. For decisions below the highest stakes, phone is efficient and often preferable to the time cost of in-person meetings.

Written (email, letter, memo): For documentation, not persuasion. Written communication creates records — which is valuable. But it strips out all the real-time intelligence that closes deals. Do not use email to negotiate. Use email to confirm what you negotiated in person.

McCormack's memo habit: after every important meeting, he wrote a brief summary of what was agreed and sent it to all parties. Not bureaucracy — insurance. "I never said that" conversations derail more deals than bad negotiating positions. The paper trail is protection.

Negotiation — The Game Within the Deal

McCormack's battle-tested rules from 40 years at the highest-stakes tables on earth
Street Intelligence

McCormack negotiated with every major broadcaster, sports federation, corporation, and sponsor on earth. His philosophy cuts against academic negotiation theory in one critical way: he does not pretend that negotiation is primarily a rational exchange of interests. It is — but it happens through people, with all their ego, fear, pride, and irrationality. The rational model tells you what the deal should look like. McCormack tells you how to actually get there through real human beings.

R3
The Six Non-Negotiable Rules
McCormack's core negotiation framework — distilled from 40 years across every industry and culture.
▾ expand
Rule 1 — Know Your Walk-Away Before You Sit Down: Define the point below which you will not go before the negotiation starts. This is not a negotiating position you'll reveal — it's a private number that protects you from making bad deals under pressure. Without it, the pressure of the room will set your walk-away for you, and it will always be lower than it should be.

Rule 2 — Let Them Go First: The first number stated anchors the negotiation psychologically. Both parties will negotiate relative to that anchor, even when they explicitly try not to. Making them anchor first is a structural advantage. Their number also tells you their range — information you cannot get any other way.

Rule 3 — Never Accept the First Offer: Even if the first offer is exactly what you wanted. Accepting immediately signals either that you asked too little, or that they feel they overpaid. Both undermine the relationship and the execution of the deal. Make them earn it — even if earning it costs them almost nothing.

Rule 4 — Understand Their Constraints: What looks like resistance is often a structural constraint — internal budget cycles, approval thresholds, competing commitments, political considerations. Understanding the constraint lets you work around it rather than against it. "Our board won't approve more than X without a committee vote" is not a negotiating position — it's information about how to structure the deal.

Rule 5 — Concede Slowly and Visibly: Concessions made too quickly or too easily are discounted — the other party doesn't register them as real gives. Every concession you make should feel earned by the other party and visible to them: "I'm pushing hard internally to make this work for you." Slow, visible concession creates reciprocity pressure.

Rule 6 — Play the Long Game: Most negotiations are not isolated transactions. They are episodes in ongoing relationships. Winning a negotiation by leaving the other party feeling beaten is a short-term gain with long-term relationship cost. The goal is a deal both parties want to execute — and will execute with energy, not resentment.
Advanced Tactic
The Disappearing Offer
When the other party stalls without clear reason, McCormack would occasionally withdraw the offer — not permanently, but with a stated time limit. Loss aversion kicks in harder when something is being removed than when something is being offered. Use sparingly. Only works with a credible withdrawal.
Advanced Tactic
The Comfort Zone Close
Deals close where the other party feels safe. McCormack often chose to finalize deals in the other party's office — on their turf. A comfortable, respected counterpart makes faster, more generous decisions. Pressure environments make people defensive, slow, and small. Counter-intuitive but consistently effective.
Advanced Tactic
The Strategic Underreveal
Open by understating your position slightly. Let the other party feel they've made progress. Then reveal the fuller ask as a logical escalation. They've already mentally committed to a partial deal — pulling back feels like loss, not as a new demand being added.
Advanced Tactic
The Unsaid Leverage
Never reveal the full extent of what you need from them. The moment you expose your dependency, they have leverage. Give enough to build trust and move the conversation. Withhold enough to maintain balance. This is not deception — it's negotiation discipline.

Running Things — What Managers Actually Do

McCormack's unsentimental guide to managing people, time, and the theater of organizations
Street Intelligence

McCormack's management philosophy comes entirely from doing, not from theory. Many of his insights conflict directly with the HR orthodoxy of his era — and remain more honest than much of what passes for leadership wisdom today.

R4
Hire Smart. Define Outcomes. Get Out of the Way.
McCormack's management philosophy in one sentence — and why it's harder than it sounds.
▾ expand
Hire people smarter than you in their domain. This requires sufficient self-awareness to know what your domain is, and sufficient ego security to hire people who will overshadow you in theirs. Many managers hire people slightly worse than themselves — people who won't challenge or replace them. This is how organizations slowly calcify.

Define outcomes, not process. Tell people what winning looks like. Give them the authority and resources to get there. Hold them accountable to the result — not the method. The moment you specify the method, you've taken ownership of the outcome. And you've communicated distrust.

Delegate outcomes, not tasks. A task delegation says "do this." An outcome delegation says "make this happen." The difference: outcome delegation preserves the subordinate's agency, creativity, and accountability. Task delegation creates an executor with no skin in the game and no reason to think beyond the specific instruction.

The entrepreneur's trap: The founder or senior executive who cannot let go of execution detail becomes a bottleneck for every decision. They signal — loudly — that they don't trust their team. Talented people stop initiating because they know it will be overridden. The organization becomes the leader's shadow instead of its own entity.
McCormack's Time Rules — The Only Non-Renewable Resource
Agenda or declineNever attend a meeting without an agenda. Every minute you spend in a meeting is a minute you are purchasing. Know what you're buying.
Hardest task firstDo the most important, most difficult task before email, before calls, before meetings. After that, everything else is easier or doesn't matter.
Delegate everything elseIf it doesn't require your specific judgment, relationships, or credibility — delegate it. Ruthlessly. The cost of not delegating is paid in your most valuable hours.
Phone over emailA five-minute call resolves what a five-day email chain cannot. Email is for documentation. Phone is for decisions.
Dead time is thinking timeFlights, waiting rooms, commutes — McCormack used every dead minute for thinking, planning, and relationship maintenance. Never waste it.
Busy ≠ productiveThese are opposites in disguise. The busiest people are often the least productive. Protect your calendar the way you'd protect a financial asset — because it is one.
The most dangerous manager is the one who mistakes activity for progress. Being in meetings all day, responding to emails instantly, always available — this is not high performance. It is the performance of high performance.
— McCormack's observation on organizational theater

Timing, Power & The Invisible Game

Reading situations. Knowing when to move. Understanding who actually holds power in any room.
Street Intelligence

McCormack's most underrated insight: the when of a decision is often more consequential than the what. The same proposal, delivered at the wrong moment, fails. Delivered at the right moment — when the other party has a fresh problem you solve, or when they've just celebrated a win and are expansive, or when a competitor has just disappointed them — succeeds with far less friction.

R5
Power Mapping — Who Actually Decides
Formal titles frequently misrepresent real power. The person who signs the contract is often not the person who made the decision.
▾ expand
The shadow hierarchy: In any organization, there is the organizational chart, and there is the actual power structure. The executive assistant who controls calendar access. The finance director who approves or delays budget. The technical expert whose opinion the CEO privately defers to on everything within their domain. These people have enormous operational power that their titles completely obscure. McCormack made a practice of identifying and cultivating these relationships as deliberately as he cultivated relationships with named decision-makers.

Information asymmetry: McCormack was careful — deliberately, systematically careful — about what he revealed, to whom, and when. Not because he was secretive for its own sake, but because information disclosed too early frequently destroyed negotiating leverage, or committed him to a position before he was ready to commit. The discipline of knowing what not to say is as important as knowing what to say.

Power is situational, not categorical: The person who has power in one room may have none in the next. The junior associate who seems peripheral in today's meeting is tomorrow's decision-maker. The vendor you're managing today is acquired by your biggest client next year. Treat everyone as if they might matter — because in a long enough time horizon, they usually will.

Dependency is leverage, visible dependency is vulnerability: Never let someone know the full extent of what you need from them. Give enough to build trust and move the negotiation forward. Withhold enough to maintain balance. The moment your counterpart can see exactly how much you need this deal, the negotiation is structurally over.

The Complete Operator — Putting Both Books Together

Why you need both, when to deploy each, and what it looks like when someone has mastered the full spectrum

These two books are not alternatives. They are not even complements. They are two halves of a single complete education that neither one delivers alone — and that no formal institution has yet figured out how to deliver together.

⊕
The Core Distinction

Broughton gives you the grammar of business: the frameworks, the vocabulary, the analytical tools that structure complex problems. McCormack gives you the execution layer: how to move through actual human beings and organizations to turn analysis into outcomes. The world overproduces people who can do the first. It desperately underproduces people who can do both.

◆ When You Are Broughton vs. When You Are McCormack
Broughton Mode
Building the financial model. Stress-testing the strategy. Designing the organizational structure. Writing the board memo. Analyzing why the last quarter missed targets. Evaluating an acquisition. Anywhere you need to impose structure on complexity and communicate it clearly to people who don't have time to think through the details themselves.
McCormack Mode
In any room with another human being trying to make something happen. Negotiating the deal. Recruiting the key hire. Selling the strategy to a skeptical CFO. Building a relationship with a potential client across 18 months before the business is there. Reading why the meeting just shifted. Every single interaction where analysis alone cannot move the situation.
Both Simultaneously
The genuinely excellent operator — the ones who build institutions, not just careers — uses both simultaneously. They bring rigorous analysis to every strategic question, and they read the human room every time they bring that analysis into contact with other people. They know what's true, and they know how to make people believe what's true and act on it. This combination is extremely rare and extremely valuable.
The Street Edge 01
Relationship Infrastructure
Build your network before you need it. Relationships created in desperation are transactional and forgettable. Relationships built through genuine investment over time become a compounding competitive advantage that analytical skill cannot replicate.
The Street Edge 02
Follow-Through as Moat
Most people promise and underdeliver. McCormack did exactly what he said, when he said it, without being asked twice. In a world where this is genuinely rare, it is an absolute competitive advantage. Reputation for reliability compounds indefinitely.
The Street Edge 03
Bias for Action
McCormack moved fast. He made decisions with incomplete information and corrected course. The cost of inaction — missed opportunities, ceded ground, organizational drift — consistently exceeded the cost of imperfect decisions that could be revised.
The Street Edge 04
Controlled Aggression
McCormack pursued relentlessly — but with calibration. He knew when to push and when to hold, when to reveal strength and when to conceal it. Raw aggression without intelligence is self-defeating. Controlled aggression, applied precisely, is decisive.
The most dangerous executive in any room is the one who can think clearly about complex problems AND move decisively through other human beings to make things happen. HBS teaches the first. McCormack teaches the second. The market pays extraordinary premiums for whoever has both.
— Final synthesis of both books

The Glossary — 30 Terms Every Serious Reader Must Own

From EBITDA to loss aversion, defined precisely and without condescension
EBITDA
Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. Strips out financing and accounting decisions to reveal raw operational performance. The most common metric in business valuation and deal-making.
Free Cash Flow
Operating cash flow minus capital expenditures. The purest measure of value creation — cash a business generates after paying for the investments needed to maintain it. Warren Buffett's preferred lens.
DCF
Discounted Cash Flow. A valuation method that projects future cash flows and discounts them to present value using a rate that reflects risk. Philosophically rigorous; practically sensitive to assumptions.
ROIC
Return on Invested Capital. The ratio of operating profit to capital invested. If ROIC exceeds the company's cost of capital, it is creating value. If not, it is destroying it — regardless of what the income statement says.
Leveraged Buyout (LBO)
An acquisition financed primarily with debt. The acquired company's cash flows service the debt. The equity investor controls a large asset with a small equity investment, amplifying both potential returns and potential losses.
Competitive Moat
A structural, durable advantage that protects a business's market position and profits from competitive erosion. Sources: brand, network effects, switching costs, cost advantages, regulatory licensing.
Disruption
Per Christensen: a process by which a product or service initially serving low-end or overlooked segments eventually moves upmarket and displaces established competitors. Distinct from sustaining innovation (making existing products better).
Porter's Five Forces
A framework for analyzing the structural profitability of an industry: threat of new entrants, supplier power, buyer power, threat of substitutes, and competitive rivalry. High forces = structurally low margins for everyone.
Positioning
The mental slot a brand occupies in a customer's mind. Positioning is not what you do to a product — it's what you do to the prospect's perception. The clearest and most durable competitive advantage in consumer markets.
STP
Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning. The three-step marketing strategy process: divide the market into segments, choose which to serve, and define the position you'll occupy in their minds.
Loss Aversion
The psychological phenomenon where the pain of losing something outweighs the pleasure of gaining the equivalent. Kahneman's research suggests losses feel roughly twice as powerful as gains of the same magnitude.
Anchoring
The tendency to rely disproportionately on the first piece of information offered when making decisions. In negotiation: whoever states a number first anchors the entire conversation, even when both parties try to ignore it.
Working Capital
Current assets minus current liabilities. Measures a company's short-term financial health and operational efficiency. Shrinking working capital can signal an approaching liquidity crisis before it appears in profit figures.
Authentic Leadership
Bill George's framework: leaders who know who they are, operate from clear values, and build trust through consistency. Authenticity is not transparency — it is being predictably, reliably yourself so people can trust and follow you.
Job-to-be-Done
Christensen's insight: customers don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. The drill bit is hired to make holes. Understanding the underlying job reveals competitors you'd never identify by looking at your own product category.
Control Premium
The premium an acquirer pays above a company's market price to gain operational control. Typically 20–40%. Reflects the value of being able to direct the company's strategy, management, and capital allocation without shareholder approval.
Capital Structure
The mix of debt and equity a company uses to finance its assets. Debt is cheaper (tax-deductible) but creates fixed obligations. The optimal structure maximizes value without making the company fragile.
Cost of Capital
The rate of return a company must earn to satisfy its investors (both debt and equity holders). Used as the discount rate in DCF. A business only creates value when its returns exceed its cost of capital.
BATNA
Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement. Your walk-away option. The stronger your BATNA, the more leverage you have. The first strategic step of any negotiation is improving your BATNA before entering the room.
Epistemic Humility
The recognition that your knowledge is incomplete, your models are approximations, and your conclusions might be wrong. The antidote to the overconfidence that HBS's case method can inadvertently manufacture.
